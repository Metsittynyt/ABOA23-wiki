Problem

This year our ABOA team’s project is about cleaning heavy metals, especially mercury, out of contaminated waters. Mercury is hazardous for the environment and individual organisms in multiple ways. The toxicity of mercury is caused by its high affinity for sulfhydryl groups, which can bind to proteins and disrupt their functions (Christakis et al., 2021). In ecosystems, mercury accumulates in large carnivores such as large predator fishes and mammals. In these animals it distracts normal development of the fetus and young individuals, decreases fertility and causes nervous disorders (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 2022). Many of these larger animals are used as food for humans which means that the mercury enriches in us causing these same issues. These problems in animals endangers the balance of ecosystems and harms biodiversity.


The origin of the mercury is most often fossil fuels or other mercury containing material that is burned (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 2023). The mercury then gets into the atmosphere and precipitates down with rain to our bodies of water. The bodies of water with high concentrations of biomass, especially marshlands, get accumulated with mercury. These aged marshlands may contain decades worth of mercury. As they are ditched for agriculture or forestry, the sulfates react with the oxygen in the atmosphere and produce acids that dissolve metals. Hence the metals that have been stored in the biomass for years spread into the environment with the runoff water.


Background and inspiration

In the beginning of this project our team got inspired by our native marshlands. Indeed our country is named after its many fens: fen + land = Finland, which also holds true in the Finnish language: suo + maa = Suomi. After some research on the topic we came to realize our idea could be utilized in promoting the wellbeing of our beloved Baltic Sea as well as purifying the waters of drained marshlands and all other mercury contaminated waters in Finland and the rest of the world to restore them.

The Finnish marshlands have been long used for commercial forestry and agriculture and hence have been drained. As marshlands develop during long periods of time, they get enriched by many toxic compounds such as heavy metals. As these swamps are ditched and drained, the toxic waters spread to the nature and contaminate it.


The Baltic Sea has been in a horrible condition for decades although many actions have been made in order to make it a safer habitat for flora, fauna and inhabitants of its coasts. Yet our solution could be the one that makes a sustainable impact in her health.


All in all our project has a versatile impact on the wellbeing of nature. Developing a sustainable and scalable solution for the remediation of the state of our national bodies of water will be all the more important as fresh water becomes a more scarce resource globally. There are also rising threats to food security therefore it’s important to ensure we can use fish and other sources of food safely and sustainably.


Also, by improving the state of these bodies of water and protecting these ecosystems maintains our biodiversity by providing healthy habitats for species and decreasing fertility and development issues.


In addition, industries that handle heavy metals could benefit from our solution. There are for example artisanal mines in which the people and the environment come into contact with heavy metals. The waters used in these mines or some factories that produce heavy metal contaminated sewage could purify the water with a on-site purification system.


Our solution

Our project aims to use cyanobacteria to bioremediate mercury-contaminated waters. To achieve that, our team will enhance the ability of cyanobacteria to transform toxic organic mercury into less harmful elemental mercury. This conversion is significant as compared to other organomercurials, elemental mercury is less hazardous to organisms and the environment (Dash & Das, 2012).


To reach our goal our team will genetically modify cyanobacteria, specifically Synechocystis sp. PCC6803, by overexpressing a merA- and incorporating a merB -gene to its genome. Together, MerA and MerB work synergistically to enhance the bioremediation of mercury. MerA reduces inorganic mercuric ions to elemental mercury, while MerB aids in the degradation of organic mercury compounds. Detoxifying methylmercury begins when merB-gene codes for a protein called alkylmercury lyase whose function is to cleave bonds between a mercury atom and a carbon atom in organic mercury compounds. The mercuric reductase enzyme, coded by the merA-gene, reduces mercury ions into elemental mercury. The elemental mercury can then be transported out of the cell (Christakis et al., 2021).


Synechocystis PCC6803 has naturally a merA-gene. By transforming the cyanobacteria with a merB-gene and overexpressing the merA-gene we try to develop the modified Synechocystis into an effective tool in bioremediation of mercury. To create the construct, we are going to use One-pot Golden Gate system, where multiple DNA-fragments can be assembled together in a single reaction. The system utilizes BbsI enzyme which generates compatible DNA overhangs that can be ligated into a plasmid. First we will transform the created inserts into E. coli and after that into Synechocystis. After transforming the Synechocystis we will study the function and the mercury-modifying ability of the created construct.


The current solutions for removing mercury from the environment have disadvantages such as high energy consumption and for that reason we are working on a solution that utilizes the tools of synthetic biology. Synthetic biology has been used for mercury remediation before but our novelty is to use Synechocystis: an organism that is capable of photosynthesis. Our solution would be sustainable and scalable as it utilizes phototrophic cyanobacteria, which both is a carbon sink and uses free sunlight as energy. The system would be cheap to use especially in areas with a stable source of light and heat like the Equator or regulated spaces. As our organism would not be freed to the environment and the reactions would be done in a tank, it is possible to use the system in various areas and produce clean and safe water for all.
