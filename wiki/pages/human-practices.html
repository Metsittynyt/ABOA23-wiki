Overview of the path of MercuLess


An unleashed danger

Our idea which is now fully fleshed, took a path of evolution and understanding. Finland has around 8.7 million hectares of marshes, of which 4.7 million hectares have already been drained (Metsähallitus, the Forest Ministry of Finland). It has led to the leaching of organic matter and heavy metals into the nearby soil water bodies where they become bioavailable (Leivuori, 1998). Some of these heavy metals have infiltrated the food chains and ultimately human bodies (Luonnonvarakeskus, the Nature Resource Center). It is common knowledge that by accumulating chronically, many heavy metals disrupt some vital cellular mechanisms, leading to various systemic disorders (Jaishankar, 2014).


The quest for MercuLess began

Hence we felt the urge to save our water bodies - we just were not sure how to do it. So the quest for meaning began. We did our research and contacted many experts asking for their practical suggestions. We knew we could not develop a system to fight all the pollutants within the limited project time, so wanted to focus on the major pollutant that is wreaking havoc not only in our ecosystem but also on a global scale. Céline Arzel, one of the researchers we contacted suggested we focus on mercury pollution.


Eyes on the supervillain

So we narrowed down our research and focussed on mercury, more specifically methylmercury pollution. Marshlands are an important source of methylmercury (St. Louis, 1994). Through our research, we came to know that methylmercury is the major environmental mercury pollutant on species and because of its bioavailability it is the most toxic form of mercury known to humans. A quick Google search revealed that the annual global mercury pollution is approx. 2200 metric tons - just from various anthropogenic sources. The magnitude of it shocked us and further made us determine that we must do something about it using synthetic biology (The United States Environmental Protection Agency).


Mastering the art of war

Throughout our journey, we also delved into various other aspects that are relevant - from learning more about the marshland ecosystem, restoration strategies, mercury pollution situation & its effects on humans and other biomes in general to theoretical implementation model.


We did not walk alone

We contacted several Finnish agencies and institutes to get a complete picture of the problem and initial feedback for our solution. We had frequent conversations and meetings with experts and researchers from related fields. We also contacted fishermen and forest owners - to listen to their experiences and insights regarding pollution and discussed how our project is connected to them and vice versa. They also provided us with important information and contacts. You can read more about the discussions we had with our various stakeholders from beneath.


The hope we cherish

By reflecting on all the information and suggestions we gathered over time, we wanted to outline a project that is sustainable and has a great upscaling potential to solve major global pollution. As a student team, we are determined to pave the way for a better future for the generations to come.

Conversations with our stakeholders


In our Human Practice work we wanted to learn about the bond between our project and its different stakeholders. We had conversations which helped us understand the impact our project may have. It isn’t just scientists who create solutions; we need people who are close to the problem. Forests and waters are an important source of income for many people in Finland. Our project could help to preserve the livelihoods of these people and make the gathered food safer. MercuLess helps maintain biodiversity, safe habitat and ecosystem balance.

Why did we reach out to him?
Since we got the idea to utilize our project in waste water treatment, we needed to know more about how the wastewater is handled in Finland at the moment. We reached out to the local wastewater treatment plant (Kakolan jätevedenpuhdistamo) and got an invitation to a guided trip.

What did we learn from this?
The tour at the plant was amazing and we learned a lot about wastewater treatment. The water treatment plant collects water from the municipalities’ households and factories with the help of pump stations. When the water comes to the plant it may contain anything like trash, nitrogen, phosphorus, anything. After the water has been treated at the plant, it will be directed to the sea. The sludge will first go to a biogas factory and the outproducts from that process will be used for green infrastructure such as planting plants by roads. The sludge will first be hygienisized (living cells are killed). Any solids will be burned for energy. The water will be purified mechanically of solids and biologically from any dissolved waste such as nitrogen and phosphorus. The bacteria accumulate the dissolved waste. Any toxic substances that are accumulated by the bacteria will go to the environment with the sludge. The purification process and the use of the end products do not have a standard protocol in Finland. In the biological process there is no knowledge of the content of the water: it may contain anything. The main goal of the process is to get nitrogen out of the water. The main responsibility with the heavy metal emissions lie on the factories that produce that kind of emissions; they need to clear the water from those before it comes to the wastewater plant.

What kind of impact did this have to our project?
We realized that there are many emissions that are not requuired to be taken care of. New ways of water purification are needed and our project could be one of those. We need to make people realize how harmful heavy metal, especially mercury, emissions are to the environment and then maybe someday the Finnish emission law will tighten .

Why did we reach out to him?
We reached out to the Eläinoikeuspuolue to understand their views on fishing and animal rights. From 2016 Eläinoikeuspuolue has been a registered political party which aims to make Finland a society where equal rights for fulfilling life and secured future are guaranteed for humans and other animals. Jaakko Perttunen, a board member of the party, answered our email and shed light on their opinions. Jaakko was glad to hear that we want to consider the critical side of fishing also in our project and says that it is good to bring up the viewpoints of other animals in addition to humans.

What did we learn from this?
Jaakko told us that the Eläinoikeuspuolue opposes all killing of animals, which also includes fish. They want stricter laws for fishing because the loose commercial fishing rules are one reason why the fish populations have crashed and marine ecosystems are endangered. Although they do not endorse fishing, they believe that as long as fish is used for the food of humans and pets it should be safe from mercury. The Eläinoikeuspuolue also spreads information regarding fish and aims to break myths and flawed beliefs about fish such as them being lesser animals than mammals. Regarding mercury, Jaakko said that it is great that we are creating a solution to purify it from our environment because mercury is as dangerous to other animals as it is to humans. He also said that regardless of the use of fish as a food source, it is still right to clean the mercury out of nature for the sake of the animals themselves.

What kind of impact did this have to our project?
Learning more about animal rights helped us consider them even better in our project by greatly emphasizing that both mercury and MercuLess can impact all organisms, not just humans. Also in our Implementation page we consider the risk of increasing the loosely regulated commercial fishing and the negative impacts it has to marine ecosystems. After pondering our project goals we still agreed as a team to have protecting the safe use of fish and other marine food sources as our value. We want to protect the safe use of these food sources for all species that consume fish.

Our values


Our values are things that are important for us in everyday life. Through our project we kept our team values as a guideline. All these five values below are well thought and accepted by all our team members.

Responsibility - Our goal is to take both social and environmental responsibility into consideration in our project. We want to emphasize that biodiversity, the health of ecosystems and sustainable decisions are everyone’s responsibility. We all affect the future of your planet’s wellbeing and other species’ possibility to thrive. This includes social responsibility: together securing the right for everyone to access clean water and safe food. Considering our project topic, responsibility means also responsible scientific research and work with genetically modified organisms. Our PI Pauli Kallio and the whole Molecular Plant Biology Department at University of Turku had an impact on us while we were working in our laboratory at the mentioned department as they showed us the meaning of responsible scientific research and why responsibility should be one of our values.

Courage - Our project is tackling a topic, which is both scientifically and socially difficult. Many cultures and communities have long traditions of using aquatic resources. When the issue of mercury arises, they might be reluctant to admit that their traditions have been harmful. Especially in Finland the narrative often is that there is no need for change because things have been done in the same way for decades. To give them new insight to synthetic biology and its opportunities, we need courage to face the opposition. Also, our team is young and new to the iGEM community. We do not have a large security and consultation network to provide advice or financial support. We have gained every bit of knowledge and support ourselves. For these reasons our team value is courage. This value really started from us and grew stronger as we got further with the project.

Diversity - Our team is a diverse group of students from three different universities and different backgrounds. Our team members study information technology, biotechnology, biochemistry, social sciences, biomedicine and animation providing our team a diverse skill set. Our project topic also concerns all people around the world despite location, race or wealth. In addition to humans, MercuLess impacts other animals as well. With securing as diverse life as possible for each individual, we will build a more favorable future for all. The reason for our team’s diversity are the efforts the last year’s ABOA team made when recruiting us. Jonna Pohjankukka, last year’s team member who was part of the recruiting, told us that they noticed how important diverse backgrounds and skills were when they went through their iGEM journey therefore they really focused on diversity. ABOA 2022 really had an impact on our team and therefore on our values: they showed how important diversity is and why it should be one of our values.

Unity - As the heavy metal issue concerns all of the human kind, we want to unite everyone to battle the problem. Hence we have included all age groups from children at kindergarten to elderly people in our education work. When we reach out to people of all ages, we are able to unite them to support both synthetic biology and remediation of waters from toxic mercury. When brainstorming our project we looked through countless of iGEM wikis. As we were sailing through them we realized that none of those wikis was built alone. There is a team behind every wiki and there is a team behind ours too. With unity we built this webpage, with unity we did the lab work and with unity we can make a difference in this world. In addition to this there’s the unity of the iGEM community which has been such an inspiration for us.

Well-being - With our solution our team wants to increase well-being all over the world. We want to provide safe habitats for all species and a possibility to practice cultural traditions. By decreasing the mercury concentration in bodies of water, all species have safer living habitats and better possibilities for reproduction, development and life. In addition to safer living traditional livelihoods could be maintained such as fishing. Conversations with fishers around the question “What does fishing mean to you?” locked wellbeing in our list of values. These touching conversations taught us that our project has a positive personal impact on individuals: we can secure their way of living and that is what we carry throughout this project with us.
